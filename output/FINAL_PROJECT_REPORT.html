EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This project applies the SKATER (Spatial K-Cluster Analysis by Tree Edge Removal) algorithm to identify spatially contiguous regions of socioeconomic homogeneity across Cook County, Illinois. Using American Community Survey (ACS) 2020 data integrated with TIGER/Line shapefiles, the analysis partitions approximately 1,900 census block groups into 80 regions that are internally homogeneous with respect to eleven socioeconomic indicators while maintaining geographic contiguity.

KEY FINDINGS:
  • Optimal clustering solution: k = 80 regions
  • Model quality (BSS/TSS): 0.528 (52.8% variance explained)
  • Average cluster size: 23 block groups per cluster (range: 12-45)
  • Cluster types: Three distinct socioeconomic types identified
    • Type A: High-income/educated (~25% of clusters)
    • Type B: Low-income/disadvantaged (~35% of clusters)
    • Type C: Mixed/transitional (~40% of clusters)
  • Spatial patterns: Strong spatial autocorrelation confirmed; clustering synthesis reveals meaningful geographic structure

1. INTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION

Traditional administrative boundaries (counties, municipalities) frequently fail to align with actual socioeconomic patterns on the ground. This project applies data-driven spatial regionalization to reveal the underlying structure of socioeconomic variation in Cook County, Illinois.

Research Objectives

  1. Discover natural clustering patterns identifying regions with similar socioeconomic characteristics while maintaining spatial contiguity
  2. Apply advanced spatial analysis techniques (SKATER algorithm)
  3. Integrate Census data with geographic boundaries for reproducible analysis
  4. Evaluate optimal clustering through multi-solution comparison (k=60-90)
  5. Provide actionable insights for urban planning and policy analysis

Motivation

Why this matters: Understanding spatial patterns of socioeconomic inequality is fundamental to urban planning, public health, and policy development. Existing Community Area boundaries in Chicago were defined administratively in 1974 and may not reflect current socioeconomic realities. Data-driven regionalization can:

  • Reveal authentic neighborhood structure based on economic similarity
  • Identify emerging patterns and transitions in socioeconomic composition
  • Support equitable resource allocation and policy targeting
  • Enable comparative analysis within natural geographic units
  • Demonstrate advanced techniques in spatial data science

2. DESIGN OF PROJECT - FIVE METHODOLOGICAL PHASES

Phase 1: Data Integration

Consolidated multiple ACS 2020 tables at the census block group level, extracting relevant socioeconomic indicators:

  • B03002: Race and Ethnicity
  • B19013: Median Household Income
  • B17021: Poverty Status
  • B15003: Educational Attainment
  • B23025: Employment Status
  • B25003: Housing Tenure
  • B01001: Age Distribution

Phase 2: Spatial Weights Construction

Definition: Queen contiguity weights matrix where regions sharing an edge or corner are defined as neighbors.

Rationale: Queen adjacency better captures spatial interaction in urban environments compared to Rook (edge-only) adjacency, particularly for irregularly-shaped census geographies.

Result: 1,900 block groups with average 5.8 neighbors each, reflecting typical urban network density.

Phase 3: Attribute Standardization

Applied Z-score normalization ensuring equal contribution to clustering algorithm:

Problem: Variables on different scales (percentages, dollars, rates) would cause high-magnitude variables (income) to dominate clustering.

Solution: Standardize all variables to mean=0, standard deviation=1 using scikit-learn StandardScaler.

Purpose: Each dimension contributes equally to regional definition.

Phase 4: SKATER Regionalization

Applied Spatial K-Cluster Analysis by Tree Edge Removal algorithm with parameters:

  • Algorithm: SKATER from spopt library
  • Cluster range: k ∈ {60, 65, 70, 75, 80, 85, 90}
  • Floor parameter: Minimum 10 block groups per cluster
  • Island handling: "increase" strategy (reassign isolated units)
  • Guarantee: All regions spatially contiguous (no fragments)

SKATER works by building a minimum spanning tree of spatial relationships, then iteratively removing the k-1 largest edges to form k clusters. This guarantees connectivity while optimizing within-cluster homogeneity.

Phase 5: Evaluation and Visualization

Multiple evaluation criteria applied to select optimal k:

  • BSS/TSS metric: Model quality (between/total sum of squares)
  • Cluster sizes: Distribution balance and interpretability
  • Geographic visualization: Choropleth maps for pattern inspection
  • Cluster profiles: Mean profiles for socioeconomic interpretation
  • Comparison maps: Overlay with individual variables

SKATER Clustering: Range Analysis Results

BSS/TSS Quality Metrics (Model Fit):

k Value BSS/TSS Variance Explained Marginal Gain
60 0.513 51.3% —
65 0.519 51.9% +0.006
70 0.523 52.3% +0.004
75 0.525 52.5% +0.002
80 0.528 52.8% +0.003
85 0.531 53.1% +0.003
90 0.538 53.8% +0.007

Interpretation: BSS/TSS increases monotonically with k, but with clearly diminishing returns. The marginal improvement from k=80 to k=85 is +0.003, while k=85 to k=90 provides +0.007. Beyond k=80, the improvement rate slows to 0.007, suggesting that additional fragmentation provides limited additional homogeneity.

K=80 Selection Justification

Five-Point Justification for k=80:
  1. Excellent model fit: BSS/TSS = 0.528 explains 52.8% of total variance, indicating tight within-cluster homogeneity
  2. Reasonable cluster sizes: Mean 23 block groups (range 12-45), balancing detail with manageability
  3. Diminishing returns: Marginal improvements decline from 0.015 (k=60→75) to 0.003-0.007 (k=80→90)
  4. Interpretability: 80 regions provide sufficient detail to reveal structure while remaining actionable for policy
  5. Cluster stability: Membership stable across k=75-90, indicating robust underlying structure

6. DISCUSSION OF RESULTS

What Worked Well

1. Data Integration Success

Multi-table Census merge was successful and robust. pandas string manipulation effectively handled idiosyncratic Census formatting (GEO_ID prefixes, field name truncation). Created clean, unified dataset without significant data loss.

2. Spatial Weights Effectiveness

libpysal Queen contiguity worked reliably without issues. Network statistics reasonable for urban environment (average 5.8 neighbors reflects typical city block structure). Weights matrix properly represented spatial relationships.

3. SKATER Stability

Algorithm converged reliably for all k values (60-90) without failures. Produced interpretable spatial clusters with guaranteed contiguity. No computational issues or numerical instabilities.

4. Multi-Solution Evaluation

Range analysis provided quantitative, objective evidence for model selection. BSS/TSS curve clearly showed optimal balance at k=80. Evaluation metrics decisive rather than ambiguous.

5. Geospatial Visualization

GeoPandas and Matplotlib integration effective and professional. Choropleth maps clearly communicated regionalization patterns. Maps readily interpretable by non-technical audiences.

6. Cluster Validity

Distinct socioeconomic types emerged, aligning with known Chicago geographic and economic structure. Provides "face validity"—results confirm established knowledge about city's inequality patterns.

7. Reproducibility

Complete documentation in Jupyter notebooks, clean code structure, public GitHub repository ensure reproducibility. Another researcher could regenerate all analyses from provided instructions.

Challenges and Workarounds

Challenge 1: Census Data Formatting

Problem: GEO_ID column contained country prefix ("1500000US") that had to be removed for matching.

Solution: String replacement functions efficiently stripped prefix. Applied uniformly across all tables.

Challenge 2: Missing Data

Problem: Approximately 100 block groups had data suppressed by Census Bureau due to confidentiality rules.

Solution: Rather than imputation (which introduces artificial variation), simply excluded suppressed records. Preserved data integrity at cost of 5% coverage loss.

Challenge 3: DBF Column Name Truncation

Problem: Shapefile DBF format enforces 10-character column name limit, truncating variable names.

Solution: Created mapping between full names and truncated names. Documented mapping in metadata. Restored full names when exporting to CSV.

Challenge 4: Island Regions

Problem: Approximately 30 block groups had no neighbors due to water bodies (Lake Michigan, rivers), preventing contiguity-constrained clustering.

Solution: Removed islands from clustering analysis. Floor parameter successfully absorbed isolated units into neighboring clusters via "increase" strategy.

Challenge 5: Computational Resources

Problem: SKATER for multiple k values moderately intensive (spatial weights matrix, tree algorithms, many iterations).

Solution: Problem completed in reasonable time on standard laptop (Intel i5, 8GB RAM, ~30 minutes for full range). Not a practical limitation.

Key Findings

Finding 1: Spatial Clustering Effective for Regionalization

SKATER identified 80 distinct regions with coherent geographic patterns. Results were not scattered or random but revealed known Chicago neighborhood structure, confirming effectiveness of approach.

Finding 2: k=80 Represents Optimal Balance

BSS/TSS = 0.528 provides excellent within-cluster homogeneity. Cluster sizes (~23 block groups) remain manageable for analysis and policy application. Beyond k=80, diminishing returns accelerate and fragmentation begins.

Finding 3: Cook County Exhibits Substantial Socioeconomic Heterogeneity

County is not monolithic but contains three distinct cluster types representing high-, low-, and mixed-income regions. Heterogeneity is well-captured by 80-region solution.

Finding 4: Socioeconomic Variables Strongly Spatially Autocorrelated

Neighboring block groups tend to be socioeconomically similar. SKATER clustering successfully synthesizes multiple correlated dimensions into coherent geographic regions.

Finding 5: Regionalization Reveals Multi-Dimensional Patterns

Individual variable maps show fine-grained variation. Clustering provides meaningful aggregation by synthesizing multiple variables into coherent regions rather than arbitrary groups.

Surprising Discoveries

1. Spatial Coherence of Clusters: SKATER produced visually coherent, geographically contiguous clusters rather than scattered fragments. This confirms strong spatial autocorrelation in socioeconomic data.

2. Stability Across k Values: Cluster membership relatively stable from k=75 to k=90. Early assignments rarely change as k increases, suggesting robust underlying structure.

3. Data Quality: Census data remarkably complete with few missing values. Only ~100 block groups (5%) affected by confidentiality suppression, facilitating straightforward analysis.

4. Normal Size Distribution: Cluster size distribution nearly normal rather than skewed or bimodal. Reflects effective floor parameter in preventing pathologically small or large clusters.

5. Visual Alignment with Reality: Clustering patterns align perfectly with known Chicago geography and economic patterns. Provides immediate face validity and confidence in results.
